#### **ã€Origin and History: Cormorant Feathers and a Mother's Loveã€‘**

The founding myth of Udo Shrine is the story of 'Yamasachihiko (Luck of the Mountains)' and 'Toyotamahime (Princess of the Sea),' one of the most romantic and sorrowful tales in Japanese mythology.

Long ago, Yamasachihiko, having lost his brother's fishhook, ventured deep into the sea to the Dragon Palace to find it. There, he met and fell in love with the beautiful Toyotamahime, the daughter of the sea god (Watatsumi). After three dreamlike years in the Dragon Palace, Yamasachihiko returned to the surface with the lost fishhook and jewels that could control the seas.

Sometime later, a heavily pregnant Toyotamahime came ashore to find her husband. "I could not bear to give birth to a descendant of the heavenly gods in the cold sea, so I have come here."
The couple began to build a birthing hut (ç”£å±‹) inside a cave. Just as they were about to finish thatching the roof with the feathers of a sea bird, the cormorant (éµœ, u), labor began earlier than expected, and the baby was born before the roof was complete. The deity's name, 'Ugayafukiaezu (cormorant-feather-roof-not-completed),' originates from this urgent and mystical moment of birth.

Just before giving birth, Toyotamahime earnestly pleaded with her husband.
"I must now return to my original form to have this child. Please, I beg you, do not look inside."
However, unable to overcome his curiosity and worry, Yamasachihiko broke the taboo and peeked into the cave. There, he saw not his beautiful wife, but a giant shark (or crocodile, or dragon) writhing as it gave birth.

Deeply ashamed and saddened at having her true form seen, the princess left her newborn child behind, sealed the passage between the sea and the land, and returned to the Dragon Palace forever. But a mother's heart could not leave her baby. For her hungry child, she attached both of her breasts to a rock inside the cave. From that rock, a nourishing, milk-like substance flowed, allowing the baby to grow up healthy.

The site of this poignant legend of maternal love is the very location of the current **main hall of Udo Shrine**. This place, where the sorrow and love of myth coexist, has soothed the hearts of countless people for over a thousand years.

### 2. ğŸŒŠ A Stroll Through the Grounds: Descending the Cliff to Meet the Cave

The pilgrimage route at Udo Shrine is the opposite of that at a typical shrine. While shrines are usually built 'upward' toward a mountain, Udo Shrine has a 'downward' structure (kudarimiya, ä¸‹ã‚Šå®®) that descends along a cliff. This unique form makes it one of the three great downward-sloping shrines in Japan, along with Nukisaki Shrine in Gunma and Kusakabe Yoshimi Shrine in Kumamoto.

#### **The Overwhelming Red Main Hall and the Harmony of the Cave**

From the parking lot, cross a red-painted bridge and follow the stone steps down through lush subtropical vegetation, and an incredible sight unfolds before your eyes. Beneath the cliffs where rough waves crash, a massive natural sea cave appears, as if its mouth is wide open.

Inside that cave rests the vibrant vermilion main hall (æœ¬æ®¿). The contrast between the dark interior of the cave and the brilliant colors of the main hall, combined with the view of the blue sea outside, creates a sense of wonder that goes beyond mere mystery.
As you stand before the main hall with your shoes off, your ears are filled only with the magnificent sound of the waves. The cave acts as a natural resonance chamber, amplifying the sound. A prayer offered with this sound of nature as a backdrop resonates more deeply than ever before.

#### **Ochichiiwa (ãŠä¹³å²©): A Mother's Unending Love**

If you go around to the back of the main hall, you can see the **'Ochichiiwa (Breast Rock)'** from the myth with your own eyes.
Just as the legend tells, clear water still continuously drips from the protruding part of the rock. Since ancient times, it has been believed that drinking this water ensures a good supply of breast milk and helps children grow up healthy. Although it is now difficult to drink the water directly for hygiene reasons, the sight of worshippers praying for their children's health or a safe delivery as they gaze at the rock is still filled with earnestness.

Udo Shrine's famous specialty, **'Ochichi-ame (ãŠä¹³ã‚ã‚)'**, is made using this sacred water (Ochichi-mizu). This candy, with its gentle sweetness of ginger and starch syrup, is cherished as the best gift and charm for expectant or nursing mothers.

#### **Kameishi (äº€çŸ³) and Undama (é‹ç‰) Tossing**

After your prayers, stand at the railing in front of the main hall and look down at the sheer cliff below. You will see a giant rock shaped like a turtle. This is the **'Kameishi (Turtle Rock)'**. According to legend, the turtle that Toyotamahime rode from the Dragon Palace turned to stone while waiting for the princess.

On the turtle's back, there is a small hollow marked with a rope. There is a fun ritual where it is said your wish will come true if you toss a small clay ball called **'Undama (fortune ball)'** into this hollow.

- **How to throw**: Men must throw with their left hand, and women with their right.
- **Price**: 200 yen for 5 balls.

The distance is greater than it looks, and the sea breeze is strong, making it quite challenging to succeed. But the thrill when an Undama lands in the hollow with a satisfying clink is indescribable. Even if you fail, don't be disappointed. It is said that an Undama that shatters against the rock takes your bad luck with it, which can lighten your heart.

### 4. ğŸš— Nearby Attractions & Cuisine: The Nichinan Coast Drive

The Nichinan Coast Road, where Udo Shrine is located, is a beautiful driving course so scenic it's called the 'Sun Road.' Here are some nearby attractions to visit along with the shrine.

#### **1. Sun Messe Nichinan (ã‚µãƒ³ãƒ¡ãƒƒã‚»æ—¥å—)**

A 10-minute drive from Udo Shrine. This theme park is famous for its **seven giant Moai statues** standing with the blue sea at their back. They were erected with the only permission for restoration granted by the Easter Island elders' council in the world. Each statue symbolizes a different type of luck, such as work, health, and love. The panoramic view of the Pacific Ocean from the 'Hill of the Sun' at the top of the park is breathtakingly refreshing.

#### **2. Aoshima Shrine (é’å³¶ç¥ç¤¾)**

Located about a 30-minute drive north from Udo Shrine towards Miyazaki City. The entire small island, surrounded by a bizarre wave-cut rock formation called the **'Devil's Washboard (é¬¼ã®æ´—æ¿¯æ¿)'**, is a shrine. This shrine also enshrines Yamasachihiko and Toyotamahime, making it a perfect addition to a 'mythology pilgrimage' course along with Udo Shrine. The grounds, overgrown with the subtropical plant Livistona, have an exotic atmosphere, almost as if you've stepped into Southeast Asia.

#### **3. Cuisine: Seafood at Horikawa Restaurant**

Nichinan is a port town famous for its bonito (katsuo) fishing. You can find many restaurants serving fresh seafood near Udo Shrine or around Aburatsu Port.

- **Recommended Dishes**: I recommend the 'Katsuo Aburi Ju (Seared Bonito Rice Bowl)' or Miyazaki's soul food, 'Chicken Nanban.' A rice bowl with freshly caught fish is especially delicious and will make you forget the fatigue of your journey. Try visiting local favorites like 'Horikawa Restaurant.'
