The shrine enshrines three deities: **Susanoo-no-Mikoto** (warding off evil), **≈åyamakui-no-kami** (mountains and industry), and **Homudawake-no-Mikoto** (luck in competition).

#### **„ÄêOrigin and History: A Miraculously Preserved Treasure of Edo„Äë**

Its origins trace back 1,900 years, when Yamato Takeru first enshrined a deity here. Later, in 1706, the fifth shogun, Tokugawa Tsunayoshi, built the current magnificent main hall to celebrate the birth of his heir.
Amazingly, this place escaped the fires of both the Great Kanto Earthquake and the bombing of Tokyo. As a result, seven of its structures, including the main hall (Honden), worship hall (Haiden), and gates, are all designated as **National Important Cultural Properties**, perfectly showcasing the essence of the 'Gongen-zukuri' architectural style from the Edo period.

### 2. üå∫ Strolling the Grounds (Keidai): The Red Tunnel and the Hill of Flowers

Simply walking through the grounds feels like traveling through time.

#### **Senbon Torii (Thousand Torii Gates)**

The highlight of Nezu Shrine! This tunnel of red torii gates is reminiscent of Fushimi Inari in Kyoto. There's a belief that passing through from north to south **'wards off evil,'** while passing from south to north helps **'wishes come true.'** The tunnel, bathed in red light, is a perfect photo spot.

#### **Azalea Hill (Tsutsuji-en)**

Every year from mid-April to early May, the 'Tsutsuji-en' (Azalea Garden) on the shrine's hill is covered with 3,000 azalea bushes of 100 different varieties. The sight of the roundly trimmed azaleas spreading out like a colorful carpet is the main attraction of the 'Bunkyo Azalea Festival'.

#### **Otome Inari Shrine**

This is a small shrine located midway through the torii tunnel, overlooking a pond. It is called 'Otome (Maiden) Inari' because it is said to watch over women's **relationships (en-musubi)**.

#### **A Respite for Literary Masters**

This was also a favorite walking path for great Japanese writers like Natsume Soseki and Mori Ogai. Take a moment to sit on the 'Bungo no Ishi' (Literary Masters' Stone) within the grounds and lose yourself in thought.

### 4. üèòÔ∏è Nearby Attractions: A Stroll Through the Yanesen Alleys

After visiting the shrine, take a walk through the 'Yanesen' area (Yanaka, Nezu, Sendagi), where the old-world charm of Tokyo remains.

#### **1. Nezu no Taiyaki**

- **Location**: A 5-minute walk from the shrine.
- **Features**: One of Tokyo's top three **taiyaki** shops. The thin, crispy shell is filled to the very tip of the tail with red bean paste. (Be warned: they often sell out in the morning!)

#### **2. Yanaka Ginza Shopping Street**

- **Access**: A 15-minute walk.
- **Features**: A retro shopping street famous as 'Cat Town.' Enjoying a freshly fried menchi-katsu (minced meat cutlet) while watching the sunset from the **'Yuyake Dandan' (Sunset Stairs)** is an essential part of any Yanesen stroll.

#### **3. Udon Restaurant 'Kamachiku'**

- **Location**: Near Nezu Station.
- **Features**: An udon restaurant housed in a renovated 100-year-old stone warehouse. A bowl of chewy udon enjoyed while gazing at the garden is a work of art.
