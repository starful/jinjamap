Caught in a power struggle, he was unjustly exiled and met a tragic end, becoming known as one of Japan's three great vengeful spirits (OnryÅ). Here, however, he is worshipped as a **deity who severs bad ties**, honoring the vow he made in exile to "cut off all worldly desires and attachments."

#### **ã€Origin and History: From Curse to Salvationã€‘**

Defeated in the 'Hogen Rebellion' of 1156, Emperor Sutoku was exiled to Sanuki (modern-day Kagawa Prefecture). He devoted himself to Buddhism to sever his desires, but was utterly ignored by the imperial court and passed away in anger. Subsequently, a series of disasters struck Kyoto, and this shrine was established to appease his restless spirit.
Initially, he was worshipped out of fear, but this gradually transformed into the belief that "just as the emperor cut off all his desires, he will also sever the bad ties that trouble us," making it the 'Enkiri' sanctuary it is today.

### 2. ğŸ•³ï¸ A Stroll Through the Precincts: Reborn by Passing Through the Stone

The shrine grounds are small but filled with the fervent energy of people waiting to pass through the stone.

#### **The Stone for Severing and Tying Bonds (Enkiri Enmusubi Hi)**

This is the main attraction! A massive stone monument, 1.5 meters high and 3 meters wide. It is so completely covered in white paper charms (katashiro) that its original stone surface is invisible. In the center is a hole just large enough for one adult to pass through.

**[The Stone Crawling Ritual]**

1.  **Pray at the Main Hall**: First, pay your respects to the deity at the main hall (honden).
2.  **Write on a Katashiro**: Obtain a paper slip at the reception desk (donation of 100 yen or more) and write down the bad connection you wish to sever and the good connection you wish to form.
3.  **Sever the Bad Tie**: Holding the paper, crawl through the hole from the **front to the back**. (Parting with the bad tie)
4.  **Form the Good Tie**: Crawl back through the hole from the **back to the front**. (Forging the good tie)
5.  **Offer the Charm**: Paste your paper slip onto any spot you like on the monument using the provided glue.

The act of crawling through the narrow hole is symbolic, representing a rebirth, much like passing through a mother's womb.

#### **Kushizuka (Comb Mound)**

This is a mound where old combs and hair ornaments are laid to rest. In Japanese culture, a woman's hair is a symbol of life force and beauty, but it can also be a place where grudges (æ¨) reside. By making offerings of combs, visitors pray for beauty and women's happiness.

#### **Konpira Ema-kan (Ema Museum)**

This small museum displays a collection of ema (votive plaques) from various eras, dedicated from all over Japan. Take a look at how people's earnest wishes have been expressed through pictures.

### 4. ğŸµ Nearby Attractions: Into the Ambiance of Gion

After visiting the shrine, explore these places to soak in the traditional atmosphere of Kyoto.

#### **1. Gion Hanamikoji**

-   **Location**: 5-minute walk.
-   **Features**: Kyoto's most famous geisha district. If you're lucky, you might spot a maiko among the traditional wooden townhouses. A stroll through the serene alleys is a must!

#### **2. Kennin-ji Temple**

-   **Location**: 10-minute walk.
-   **Features**: Kyoto's oldest Zen temple. The National Treasure 'Wind God and Thunder God' screen and the massive 'Twin Dragons' painting on the ceiling of the Dharma Hall are breathtaking sights. Enjoy a moment of meditation in its beautiful gardens.

#### **3. Gion Kinana (Roasted Soybean Flour Ice Cream)**

-   **Location**: In an alley off Hanamikoji.
-   **Menu**: Famous for 'Kinana', a healthy ice cream made from mugwort and roasted soybean flour (kinako). Its gentle, smooth flavor is a delight.
