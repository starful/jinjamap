A dense forest, hard to believe it's in the middle of a city, envelops the shrine. Here are some of the special sights you can encounter while paying your respects.

#### **The Majestic Haiden and Honden: The Aesthetics of Shinmei-zukuri**

The *haiden* (worship hall) you encounter after passing through the *sandÅ* (approach) was rebuilt in 2001, but it fully captures the beauty of traditional shrine architecture. The *honden* (main sanctuary) behind it is built in the **'Shinmei-zukuri (ç¥æ˜é€ )'** style, the same as Ise Grand Shrine. Offer your prayers here with 'two bows, two claps, and one bow' to cast off your inner hesitation and gain courage.

#### **A Colony of 20,000 Azaleas: The Garden of the Gods**

Another nickname for Hirota Shrine is the **'Shrine of Azaleas'**. Within the precincts and on Mount Hirota behind the shrine, there is a colony of a type of azalea called **'Kobanomitsuba Tsutsuji'**, designated as a Natural Monument of Hyogo Prefecture. Their number reaches a staggering 20,000.
Every year in mid-April, the sacred forest is dyed in a blazing crimson. This brilliant spectacle, arriving after the cherry blossoms have fallen, offers an overwhelming beauty, like a victor's crown bestowed by the gods. If you visit during this time, I highly recommend taking a walk along the trails.

Next to the *haiden*, please take note of a rock placed in a less conspicuous spot. This is the **'Sazare-ishi (pebbles)'** mentioned in the lyrics of Japan's national anthem, 'Kimigayo.' The sight of small pebbles binding together over long years to form a single large boulder symbolizes 'unity,' 'prosperity,' and 'eternity.' It is a power spot that quietly bestows strength upon athletes needing team cohesion or those wishing for family harmony.
