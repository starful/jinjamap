The main deity here is **Atsuta-no-Okami**. This refers to the divine spirit dwelling within the **'Kusanagi-no-Tsurugi (Grass-Cutting Sword).'**
This sword, obtained by the god Susanoo after defeating a monstrous serpent and offered to the sun goddess Amaterasu, symbolizes military power and authority. The other enshrined deities are also prominent figures from mythology, including Yamato Takeru (the hero who wielded the sword) and Susanoo. Because of this, the shrine is believed to hold powerful sway over **national peace**, **good fortune in battle**, and **the granting of wishes**.

The shrine's founding legend is tragic yet beautiful. The hero Yamato Takeru, on his way back from conquering the eastern provinces, fell in love with and married a woman from Owari (Nagoya) named Miyasuhime. However, he left his divine swordâ€”his other selfâ€”in his wife's care when he departed to subdue the god of Mount Ibuki.
He ultimately fell ill and passed away. The grieving Miyasuhime built a shrine to house her husband's keepsake, the divine sword. This was the beginning of Atsuta Jingu 1,900 years ago. It feels as if the poignant emotion of a shrine built in memory of a loved one permeates the entire forest.

### 2. ğŸŒ² A Stroll Through the Grounds: The Wall of Victory and the Spring of Beauty

The 60,000-tsubo (approx. 198,000 sq. meters) forest in the city center is a massive healing spot in itself. Here are some points you shouldn't miss.

#### **Nobunaga-bei Wall (ä¿¡é•·å¡€): A Miraculous Victory**

On the path to the main hall, you can see a unique wall made of layered earth and tiles. In 1560, **Oda Nobunaga** prayed here for certain victory before the 'Battle of Okehazama,' where he was overwhelmingly outnumbered. After miraculously winning, he dedicated this 'Nobunaga-bei Wall' as a token of gratitude. Walk along the wall and soak in the victorious energy.

#### **Okusu (Giant Camphor Tree): The Sacred Tree Where a Snake Lives**

Within the grounds are giant camphor trees over 1,000 years old. The **'Okusu'** in front of the main hall is said to have been planted by Kobo Daishi, and a snake is believed to live inside it. There's a popular belief that if you're lucky enough to see this snake, your **financial luck** will skyrocket, so keep your eyes peeled!

#### **Shimizusha (Okami-no-Izumi): The Secret to Beautiful Skin**

Near the forest path behind the main hall (Kokoro-no-Komichi) is a small shrine called **'Shimizusha.'** The spring behind it, **'Okami-no-Izumi,'** has a legend that washing your skin with its water will make it beautiful. They say even Yang Guifei, one of the world's three great beauties, benefited from this water! Sprinkle water on the rock in the center of the spring three times and make a wish.

### 4. ğŸ± Nearby Attractions: The Taste of Nagoya, Hitsumabushi

After visiting the shrine, indulge in Nagoya's finest cuisine.

#### **1. Atsuta Houraiken Main Store**

- **Location**: Right next to the shrine's main gate (3-min walk).
- **Features**: This is the **birthplace and original restaurant** of Nagoya's specialty, **'Hitsumabushi (grilled eel on rice).'** The eel, grilled with a 140-year-old secret sauce, is crispy on the outside and moist on the inside. Don't miss the "three-way" feast: eating it as is, with condiments, and as ochazuke (with broth poured over it). (The lines are long, so make a reservation or get a numbered ticket in advance!)

#### **2. Shirotori Garden**

- **Location**: 10-min walk.
- **Features**: The largest Japanese-style garden in the Chubu region. The seasonal flowers, autumn leaves, and the 'Seiutei' tea house, which seems to float on the water, are beautiful. Here you can experience a refined natural beauty distinct from the shrine's forest.

#### **3. Nagoya Castle**

- **Travel**: 15 minutes by subway.
- **Features**: Nagoya's landmark, with its shining golden shachihoko (tiger-fish). If you felt ancient times at Atsuta Jingu, here you can experience the splendid Edo period.
