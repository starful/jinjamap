### 1. üôè Deities and Origins: Ebisu-sama, the Bundle of Joy from Across the Sea

Before passing through the shrine's red torii gate, shall we first learn about its master, Ebisu-sama?

#### **„ÄêEnshrined Deity (Âæ°Á•≠Á•û): Kotoshironushi-no-mikoto (Ebisu)„Äë**

The main deity (‰∏ªÁ•≠Á•û) here is **Kotoshironushi-no-mikoto**, also known as **Ebisu-sama**.
Yes, it's him‚Äîthe one with a fishing rod in his right hand and a large sea bream in his left, beaming with a wide smile. Originally the god of fishing prayed to for a bountiful catch, in commercially developed Osaka, he is more famous as the **'God of Business'** and the **'God of Wealth'**.
Although other prominent deities like Amaterasu (the sun goddess) and Susanoo (averter of calamities) are also enshrined, the main star here is undoubtedly the friendly Ebisu-sama.

#### **„ÄêOrigins and History: Prince Shotoku's Guardian Deity„Äë**

The shrine's history dates back a remarkable 1,400 years to the era of Prince Shotoku. When Prince Shotoku was building Shitennoji Temple, he established Imamiya Ebisu Shrine to protect the western direction. At that time, this area was coastal, close to fish markets, and bustling with maritime trade. Naturally, the god of fishing came to be worshipped, and as merchants from the market gradually gathered, it became a center for commercial prosperity.

The **'Toka Ebisu'** festival, which began in the Edo period, has become a source of pride and identity for Osaka's merchants. The famous slogan, "Bring your bamboo branch (sasa) for a thriving business!" still heats up January in Osaka.

### 2. üèÆ A Stroll Through the Grounds: Strike the Gong and Shout Your Wish

The shrine grounds are modest in size but filled with the fervent wishes of Osaka's merchants. Here are the special worship spots you shouldn't miss.

#### **The Majestic Haiden and its Unique Roof**

The front *haiden* (hall of worship) is both magnificent and ornate. Behind it, the *honden* (main hall) has an impressive bronze roof, which seems to symbolize the resilience of Osaka, having been rebuilt after its destruction in the war. Here, you should offer your prayers respectfully with the "two bows, two claps, one bow" method.

#### **‚òÖKey Point: The Dora (Gong) Behind the Main Hall**

Don't just leave after praying at the *haiden*! **Go around to the back of the main hall.** There, you'll find two large **dora (gongs)** hanging.
According to legend, Ebisu-sama is a bit hard of hearing. Therefore, there's a fun belief that after praying at the front, you must go to the back, strike these gongs **'Bang! Bang!'** and shout your wish loudly once more to make sure he hears you.
Try striking it vigorously while saying, "Ebessan! Don't forget my wish! Please grant it!" It's incredibly satisfying.

#### **Daikokusha, Enshrining Okuninushi**

Within the grounds, there is also a sub-shrine dedicated to **Daikokuten (Okuninushi)**, who is Ebisu's father and another god of fortune. If you pay respects to both the father (Daikoku, for wealth) and the son (Ebisu, for business), your financial luck is practically guaranteed, right?

### 4. üç§ Nearby Attractions: Osaka's Retro Vibe and Culinary Delights

After visiting the shrine, enjoy some of Osaka's famous spots that are within walking distance.

#### **1. Shinsekai & Tsutenkaku Tower**

- **On Foot**: 10 minutes.
- **Features**: A place where Osaka's retro atmosphere explodes! The flashy signboards and the towering **'Tsutenkaku'** observatory are must-sees. Don't forget to meet the 'Billiken' statue‚Äîrubbing his feet is said to bring good luck.

#### **2. Kushikatsu Food Tour**

- **Recommendation**: Shinsekai is the birthplace of **'kushikatsu' (deep-fried skewers)**. Enjoy freshly fried, crispy skewers with a cold beer at famous shops like 'Daruma' or 'Yaekatsu'. Just remember to follow the "no double-dipping" (nidotsuke kinshi) rule!

#### **3. Den Den Town**

- **On Foot**: 5 minutes.
- **Features**: Osaka's Akihabara! It's a paradise for fans of figurines, anime merchandise, and electronics. Try your luck at finding a great deal with the fortune you received at the shrine.
