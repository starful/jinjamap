The history of Futarasan Shrine is the history of Nikko itself. Let's turn the clock back 1,200 years to the Nara period.

In 766, a young monk named **Shodo Shonin (å‹é“ä¸Šäºº)** was drawn to the sacred mountains of Nikko. However, his path was blocked by the fiercely roaring rapids of the Daiya River (å¤§è°·å·). As he stood before a river that seemed impossible for any human to cross, he prayed earnestly.

Then, a miracle occurred. From the opposite bank, a giant in the form of a god, 'Jinja-Daio,' appeared and threw a red and a blue snake, which he had coiled around his arm, into the river. The two snakes intertwined, forming a beautiful bridge like a rainbow, and mountain hydrangeas bloomed on their backs. Shodo Shonin was able to cross this mystical 'serpent bridge' and enter the Nikko mountains. This is the legend of the **Shinkyo Bridge (ç¥æ©‹)**, which is today a symbol of Nikko and a UNESCO World Heritage site.

After entering the mountains, Shodo Shonin built a shrine on the summit of Mt. Nantai in 782 to worship the mountain god, marking the beginning of Futarasan Shrine. In other words, this is the true source of the 'City of Light,' Nikko.

Later, the shrine was revered by the samurai of the Kamakura Shogunate, and during the Edo period, it was developed into its current form when Toshogu Shrine, which enshrines Tokugawa Ieyasu, was built nearby. However, long before the magnificent Toshogu was established, it was Futarasan Shrine that ruled the spirit of Nikko.

### 2. ğŸŒ² A Stroll Through the Precincts: The Forest of Myths and Power Spots

The precincts of Futarasan Shrine are more than just a path for worship. Within its vast sacred grounds (ç¥åŸŸ) spanning 3,400 hectares, powerful spots brimming with life force are hidden.

#### **Main Hall (Honden) and Worship Hall (Haiden): The Dignity of Momoyama Architecture**

Following the path from beside Toshogu Shrine, a large bronze torii gate appears. The **Worship Hall (Haiden)** visible beyond it was a building donated in 1619 by the second shogun, Tokugawa Hidetada. Unlike the opulence of Toshogu, it is a masterpiece of Momoyama period architecture that showcases the profound texture of unlacquered wood.

The **Main Hall (Honden)** behind the Haiden is a National Important Cultural Property. While general worshipers cannot get close, you can convey your prayers from the front of the Haiden using the "two bows, two claps, and one bow" etiquette. You will feel a sense of peace as if the tranquil forest air is listening to your prayers.

#### **Iconic Structure: Shinkyo Bridge (ç¥æ©‹), Crossing the Serpent Bridge**

A short distance from the main shrine, at the entrance to the Daiya River, stands the beautiful vermilion **Shinkyo Bridge**. This is the site of Shodo Shonin's 'serpent bridge legend' mentioned earlier and is considered one of Japan's three finest bridges.
In the past, it was a sacred domain that only priests or the shogun could cross, but now anyone can cross it for an admission fee. The view of the blue stream in the valley and the surrounding greenery from the bridge is truly spectacular. It's also a perfect spot for a commemorative photo to mark the start of your Nikko journey.

1. **Couple Cedar (Meoto-sugi) & Parent-Child Cedar (Oyako-sugi)**
    Next to the main hall, there is a 'Couple Cedar,' where two cedar trees are joined as one from the roots, and a 'Parent-Child Cedar,' where three trees stand side by side. Befitting a shrine that enshrines family gods, it is said that praying before these trees will improve marital harmony and bring peace to the family. Feel the warm energy of the thousand-year-old trees.

3. **Sacred Tree of Matchmaking (Enmusubi-no-Goshiboku)**
