Each red pillar of Fushimi Inari holds 1,300 years of stories.

#### **„ÄêEnshrined Deity (Âæ°Á•≠Á•û): Ukanomitama, the God of Abundance„Äë**

The main deity (‰∏ªÁ•û) here is **Ukanomitama-no-Okami**. 'Uka' means grain, or rice.
Originally a god of agriculture, as times changed, the deity evolved into the **'God of Business'** and the **'God of Wealth,'** supporting prosperity in all industries, including commerce and manufacturing. That's why it has become the most visited sacred site for Japanese entrepreneurs and merchants.
Thanks to the co-enshrined deities Sarutahiko (transportation) and Omiyanome (performing arts), it's also a great place to pray for **safety** and success in the **arts**.

#### **„ÄêOrigin and History: The Rice Cake that Became a Swan and Flew Away„Äë**

In 711, a wealthy man of this region, Hata no Irogu, committed an arrogant act of using a rice cake (mochi) as a target for archery practice. When shot, the rice cake transformed into a white swan and flew to Mount Inari, and where it landed, rice stalks (ine) grew (nari).
Regretting his actions, Irogu built a shrine there and enshrined the god of grains. This marked the beginning of the **'Inari (Á®≤Ëç∑)'** shrine. Since then, it has grown into Kyoto's premier shrine, revered by the imperial court and samurai warriors.

### 2. ü¶ä A Stroll Through the Grounds: The Red Torii Tunnel and the Wishing Stone

The shrine grounds are a vast sacred area covering the entire Mount Inari. Here are the key points you shouldn't miss.

#### **Senbon Torii (A Thousand Torii Gates)**

The symbol of Fushimi Inari! The **'Senbon Torii,'** leading from behind the main hall, is actually a tunnel formed by nearly 10,000 torii gates. These gates were donated by people whose wishes came true, as a sign of gratitude. The light filtering through the densely packed pillars creates a dreamlike atmosphere. Capture the best photo of your life.

#### **Omokaru Ishi (Heavy-Light Stone)**

Past the Senbon Torii, you'll find the **'Okusha Hohaisho' (Inner Shrine Worship Hall)**. The famous attraction here is the **'Omokaru Ishi,'** a stone placed on top of a stone lantern.

1. Make a wish.
2. Lift the stone.
3. **If it feels lighter than you expected, your wish will come true!** If it feels heavier, it's a message from the gods that more effort is needed. Will your stone feel light?

#### **Mount Inari Pilgrimage (Oyama Meguri)**

If you have the stamina, try the 4km trail to the mountain summit. It takes about 2 hours, and the spectacular view of Kyoto city from the **'Yotsutsuji'** intersection will make the effort worthwhile.

### 4. üçú Nearby Attractions: Fox Udon and a Glass of Sake

After visiting the shrine, enjoy the tastes and styles of Kyoto.

#### **1. Food on the Approach (Sando)**

- **Features**: The food stalls and shops lining the long path from the shrine entrance will whet your appetite.
- **Recommended**: **'Inari Sushi'** (sushi rice in fried tofu pouches) and **'Kitsune Udon'** (udon with fried tofu) are a must-try! They originate from the legend that the fox god loves fried tofu (aburaage). The **'Tsujiura Senbei' (the origin of the fortune cookie)**, a crispy rice cracker with a fortune inside, is also fun.

#### **2. Tofuku-ji Temple**

- **Getting there**: 15-minute walk (or 1 train stop).
- **Features**: One of Kyoto's best spots for autumn foliage. The view from the Tsutenkyo Bridge, which spans a valley amidst magnificent wooden structures, is breathtaking.

#### **3. Gekkeikan Okura Sake Museum**

- **Getting there**: 10 minutes by train (Chushojima Station).
- **Features**: Thanks to its pure water, Fushimi is home to many sake breweries. At the museum of the famous sake brand 'Gekkeikan,' you can see the brewing process and enjoy a tasting of aromatic sake.
