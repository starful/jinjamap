**The Deities of Luck and Wealth**
The shrine enshrines three primary deities:
1.  **Ukanomitama-no-mikoto (Inari):** The deity of rice and prosperity, ensuring success in business.
2.  **Ichikishimahime-no-mikoto (Benzaiten):** The goddess of water, music, and wealth, specifically honored here as "Koami Benzaiten."
3.  **Fukurokuju:** One of the Seven Lucky Gods, representing virtue, happiness, and longevity.

**A History of Miracles**
The reputation of Koami Shrine as a "Strong Luck" shrine is rooted in two historical miracles. In 1923, during the **Great Kanto Earthquake**, the shrine was completely destroyed, but the priest took the sacred mirror and escaped to a nearby bridge where hundreds of local residents had gathered. Miraculously, everyone who stayed with the priest survived the ensuing firestorms. 

Decades later, during **World War II**, young soldiers who visited the shrine to receive "Mayu-dama" (cocoon) amulets before deploying to the front lines all returned home safely. Furthermore, while the surrounding Nihonbashi district was leveled by the 1945 air raids, the shrine building miraculously remained untouched by fire. These events solidified its status as a sanctuary where "luck is so strong it defies destiny."

### 2. ‚õ©Ô∏è A Stroll Through the Precincts: A Personal Photo Diary

Stepping into Koami Shrine is a profound experience. It is located at a narrow intersection in the Kabuto-cho area (Tokyo's financial district), and the contrast between the towering modern office buildings and this ancient, dark-wood sanctuary is striking.

**The Rising and Descending Dragons**
One of the most important features to observe is the exquisite woodwork on the **Haiden (Worship Hall)**. Carved into the beams are two dragons: the **"Rising Dragon"** (Nobori-ryu) and the **"Descending Dragon"** (Kudari-ryu). The Rising Dragon is said to carry the prayers of the people to the heavens, while the Descending Dragon brings the blessings and luck of the gods back down to Earth. The detail in these carvings is rare for central Tokyo, as most wooden shrines were lost to fire.

**Zeniarai-no-i (The Money-Washing Well)**
In the corner of the precinct, you will find the sacred well of Benzaiten. Following a tradition called *Zeniarai*, visitors place their coins or even banknotes in a bamboo basket and wash them in the sacred water. It is believed that by doing so and returning the "cleansed" money to one's wallet, the money will act as a seed to attract even greater wealth and financial protection.

### 3. üìú Goshuin and Shrine Items: Cocoon Charms and Dragon Stamps

**The Mayu-dama Omamori**
The most unique item at Koami Shrine is the **Mayu-dama (Silk Cocoon) Amulet**. Made from real silk cocoons, it symbolizes the "strong thread" of fate that protects the bearer. Just as the soldiers returned safely after carrying these, they are now popular among travelers and those facing difficult surgery or life transitions.

**Goshuin and Dragon Talismans**
The **Goshuin** (holy stamp) of Koami is highly artistic, often featuring the motif of the rising dragon. During the New Year and special festival days, the shrine offers limited-edition stamps that are highly coveted. For those seeking financial luck, the **Zeniarai Omamori**‚Äîa small charm to be placed in the wallet‚Äîis a mandatory purchase after washing your money.

### 4. üåÉ What to See Nearby: The Wall Street of Tokyo

Koami Shrine is located in the **Nihonbashi Kabuto-cho** district, the birthplace of modern Japanese finance.
*   **Tokyo Stock Exchange:** A 5-minute walk away, this is the heart of Japan's economy. The energy of "money" and "luck" in this area is palpable, which is why so many traders and bankers visit Koami Shrine daily.
*   **Bank of Japan:** Further west, you can see the grand Western-style architecture of Japan's central bank.
*   **KITEKO:** A new lifestyle complex in Kabuto-cho featuring artisanal bakeries and bistros, perfect for a modern lunch in a historic financial setting.
