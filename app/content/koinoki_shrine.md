#### **ã€Origin and History: A Familial Love Spanning a Thousand Yearsã€‘**

Not many people know that the origin of this lovely shrine actually began with a heart-wrenchingly sorrowful farewell and longing. To find the roots of Koinoki Shrine, we must look into the life of the main deity of 'Mizuta Tenmangu' to which it belongs, **Sugawara no Michizane (è…åŸé“çœŸ)**.

Michizane, the greatest scholar and politician of the Heian period, suffered the misfortune of being demoted overnight from Kyoto to Dazaifu in Kyushu due to the slander of his political rivals. At the time, being sent to Dazaifu was essentially a sentence of death by exile. What tormented him most was the fact that he had to leave his beloved wife and young children behind in Kyoto and depart alone.

Life in the unfamiliar land of Dazaifu was solitary. He never forgot his family, not even for a moment until his death, and would often gaze at the Kyoto sky to soothe his piercing longing. His famous poem, *"When the east wind blows, send me your fragrance, O plum blossoms. Although your master is gone, do not forget the spring,"* fully captures his desperate feelings for his family and hometown.

In 1231 (Kamakura period), Mizuta Tenmangu was built here to console the spirit of Michizane. It was then that Michizane's lifelong **'heart of longing and adoration for his family (æ‹ã„æ…•ã†å¾¡å¿ƒ)'** was itself separated and deified, marking the beginning of Koinoki Shrine.

In other words, Koinomikoto was not a deity that existed from the beginning of time, but rather **the embodiment of a single human's pure and noble love and longing, sublimated into a faith over a thousand years**. The legend that camellia trees, traditionally called 'koinoki (love trees),' grew naturally around the shrine adds to the romance of this mystical origin story. The countless hearts we encounter here are not mere decorations, but symbols of the purest and most powerful love that transcends time and space.

The worship hall you encounter at the end of the Koi-sando is small but elegant. A key point not to be missed here is the shrine's crest (ç¥ç´‹). The patterns adorning the hall are unmistakably heart-shaped. In fact, this is a traditional Japanese architectural pattern called **'Inome (çŒªç›®),'** which depicts a wild boar's eye. It has been used since ancient times to ward off disasters and bring good fortune, but at Koinoki Shrine, its resemblance to a heart has led to its use as a 'symbol that calls for love and happiness.' The witty and exquisite blend of tradition and modern sensibility is remarkable.

Next to the main hall, two large rocks that look as if they could withstand any storm are bound firmly together by a thick sacred rope (shimenawa). These are the **'Meotoiwa (Couple Rocks).'** Symbolizing the creator couple of Japanese mythology, these rocks represent an unbreakable bond of fate. It is said that if a couple holds hands and prays before these rocks, their love will last forever.

Beside them is an adorable stone gourd statue, the **'Koimusubi Hyotan.'** Stroking this gourd, which resembles a pair of Hina dolls, is said to bring not only marital harmony but also the blessings of prosperity for descendants and safe childbirth. It's a must-visit spot for those who dream of a smooth and happy family life, much like the round curves of the gourd.

#### **ã€The Sacred Nagi Tree (ãªãã®æœ¨): An Unbreakable Bondã€‘**

Standing quietly in a corner of the grounds, the **'Nagi Tree'** is Koinoki Shrine's hidden power spot. The leaves of this tree have strong vertical veins, making them incredibly tough and difficult to tear horizontally. Because of this characteristic, it has long been regarded as a sacred tree that protects the bond of marriage, symbolizing a 'connection that cannot be broken.' A story is told of wives giving these leaves to their husbands heading to the battlefield as a token of their safe return and unchanging love. Take a deep breath under the tree and feel the power of a strong love that remains unshaken by any trial.
