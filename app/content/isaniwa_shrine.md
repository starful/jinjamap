#### **ã€Origins and History: The Miracle of Yabusameã€‘**

The origins of Isaniwa Shrine trace back to the age of mythology. It is said to have begun when Emperor Chuai and Empress Jingu visited Dogo Onsen, bathed, and were so impressed by its efficacy that they enshrined a deity on the hill. At that time, it was called "Yu-no-oka (Hill of Hot Water)" and also served as the guardian of the onsen.

However, the shrine acquired its current splendid form thanks to a dramatic event during the Edo period.
Matsudaira Sadanaga, the third lord of the Matsuyama domain, was ordered by the Edo shogunate to perform **yabusame (æµé‘é¦¬, a form of mounted archery)** in front of the shogun. This was a once-in-a-lifetime challenge with the clan's honor at stake. In this critical situation where failure could bring shame upon his family, Sadanaga visited Isaniwa Shrine and prayed earnestly.

**"If you grant me success in this competition, I will rebuild your sanctuary in splendor and offer it to you."**

Was it thanks to his prayers? He hit the targets perfectly without a single mistake. To keep his promise, in 1667, he gathered the best craftsmen of the era and completed the current magnificent sanctuary, modeled after the Iwashimizu Hachimangu in Kyoto. In other words, Isaniwa Shrine is proof of an **'absolute victory'** and a masterpiece born from the integrity of a samurai who kept his promise.

A short walk from Dogo Onsen Station, past the shopping arcade, reveals a massive stone staircase. This is where the journey into the sacred realm begins.

#### **The 135 Stone Steps and the Romon (æ¥¼é–€)**

The 135 steep steps feel like a path of ascetic training. You may find yourself out of breath with each step, but the ever-changing view of the Dogo town below offers some comfort. At the top of the stairs, a grand two-story gate, the **Romon (æ¥¼é–€)**, welcomes pilgrims. Take a moment to look back from here. It's a viewpoint offering a panoramic vista of the town, including the Dogo Onsen Main Building.

#### **National Important Cultural Property: The Aesthetics of Hachiman-zukuri (å…«å¹¡é€ )**

The precinct beyond the Romon is a world of vivid vermilion.
The most noteworthy feature is the unique structure of the main hall (Honden) and the worship hall (Haiden). It is built in the **'Hachiman-zukuri'** style, a rare form found in only three places in all of Japan.
In Hachiman-zukuri, the Honden, where the deity resides, and the Haiden, where people pray, are placed one behind the other and connected by a single massive roof, creating the appearance of twin buildings joined together. The elegant curves of the roof, the brilliant colors of the pillars, and the intricate carvings showcase the essence of Edo-period architecture. (It is counted among Japan's three great Hachiman-zukuri, along with Usa Jingu in Oita and Iwashimizu Hachimangu in Kyoto.)

#### **The Corridor (å›å»Š) and Hidden Power Spots**

The long, U-shaped corridor surrounding the main hall is also an Important Cultural Property. Take a slow walk through this corridor, where the vermilion pillars seem to stretch on endlessly.

- **Wasan Tablets**: On the corridor walls hang 'Sangaku (ç®—é¡),' wooden tablets on which people in the Edo period solved mathematical problems and dedicated them to the gods. They are not only materials showing the high intellectual level of the time but also bestow good fortune upon those wishing for academic achievement.
- **Houkaku Shrine**: A small shrine on the side of the precinct, it is a hidden gem for couples wishing for children or praying for a safe delivery.
- **Sacred Camphor Trees (æ¥ )**: In the lush forest behind the main hall, ancient camphor trees exude a sacred aura. It's a good idea to meditate for a moment while feeling the breeze from the forest.
