Do you know why Hikawa Shrine is called a sacred place for 'matchmaking'? The secret lies in the special relationship of the gods enshrined here.

#### **ã€Enshrined Deities (å¾¡ç¥­ç¥): A Family of Love and Heroesã€‘**

This is where a superstar family from Japanese mythology is enshrined.

- **Susanoo-no-Mikoto**: The god of storms and the sea, and a hero who vanquished the monstrous serpent 'Yamata no Orochi.' He wards off misfortune with his formidable power.
- **Kushinadahime-no-Mikoto**: The goddess rescued by Susanoo, who then became his wife. Their romance is one of the most dramatic love stories in mythology, symbolizing **marital harmony** and **family peace**.
- **Oonamuchi-no-Mikoto (Okuninushi)**: The descendant of the two gods and Japan's foremost **'god of matchmaking.'** He is skilled in forging all kinds of good connections, not just romantic love but also business partnerships, friendships, and more.

With such a powerful hero god, goddess of love, and god of connections gathered in one place, Hikawa Shrine is known as the ultimate power spot for those dreaming of **success in love**, **marriage**, and **mending relationships**.

#### **ã€Origin and History: A Sublime 2,400 Yearsã€‘**

The history of Hikawa Shrine dates back to 473 BC, during the reign of the 5th Emperor Kosho. For over 2,400 years, it has reigned as the guardian deity of the Kanto region. The place name 'Omiya (å¤§å®®, great shrine)' itself is derived from this shrine, a testament to its absolute influence.
It was also the first place Emperor Meiji visited for worship after moving the capital to Tokyo, and it remains a high-ranking shrine to which imperial envoys are dispatched even today.

### 2. ğŸŒ² Strolling the Grounds: Walking Japan's Longest Approach

The true essence of Hikawa Shrine can be felt from the moment you step off the train and onto the vast, forested path that begins almost immediately.

#### **Japan's No. 1 Approach (æ°·å·å‚é“)**

The approximately 2km-long path from the 'Ichi-no-torii' (first gate) to the shrine entrance is famous for being **the longest shrine approach in Japan**. The tunnel of about 650 zelkova trees, so dense they seem to cover the sky, feels as if it washes away the impurities of the mind just by walking through it. It is a beautiful walking course throughout the year, with cherry blossoms in spring, autumn leaves in fall, and deep green foliage in summer.

#### **The Sacred Red Bridge and Romon Gate (æ¥¼é–€)**

At the end of the path, a sacred red bridge (Shinkyo) appears, crossing over a holy pond. Beyond the bridge, the magnificent vermilion **Romon Gate (æ¥¼é–€)** boasts an overwhelming dignity. The moment you pass through this gate, you truly feel you have entered the divine realm, separated from the secular world.

#### **A Hidden Power Spot: The Serpent's Pond (è›‡ã®æ± )**

While most visitors only see the main hall and leave, if you want to feel the real energy, head to the forest path behind the main hall. There, you will find a mystical spring called the **'Serpent's Pond.'** This place, where water has gushed forth since ancient times, is considered the birthplace of Hikawa Shrine, and clear water still springs up today. With its quiet and cool atmosphere, it is a powerful healing spot known only to those in the know.
