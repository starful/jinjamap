The history of Toyokawa Inari is like a grand epic, tracing back about 600 years to the Muromachi period. Its beginnings lie with the renowned Kamakura period Zen master, **Kangan Giin (å¯’å·Œç¾©å°¹)**.

While studying Buddhism in Song Dynasty China, Master Kangan Giin entered a deep meditative state just before his return journey. At that moment, a mystical being appeared before him. With this promise, Dakinishinten left behind a sacred image (è–åƒ) of herself and vanished. Master Kangan Giin cherished this image, brought it back to Japan, and passed it down through generations of his disciples. Time passed, and in 1441, Kangan Giin's 6th dharma descendant, Master **TÅkai Gieki (æ±æµ·ç¾©æ˜“)**, founded a temple named 'Myogon-ji (å¦™å³å¯º)' in this land of Toyokawa. Over 1,000 fox statues, large and small, wearing red bibs, stand packed together. The foxes here are considered messengers (ä½¿è€…) of Dakinishinten. This scene was formed by a continuing tradition where people who had their wishes granted at Toyokawa Inari donate a fox statue in gratitude. Standing here, you might feel as if you can hear the whispers of the foxes, enveloped in a mysterious sensation where time seems to stand still.
