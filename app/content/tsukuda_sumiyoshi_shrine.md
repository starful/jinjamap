### 1. üôè Deities and Origins: From the Shores of Settsu to the Heart of Edo

**Tsukuda Sumiyoshi Shrine (‰ΩÉ ‰ΩèÂêâÁ•ûÁ§æ)** is not just a place of worship; it is a living monument to the birth of Tokyo's maritime culture. Its history is an extraordinary tale of migration, loyalty, and survival that dates back to the very beginning of the Tokugawa Shogunate.

**The Migration of the Fishermen**
When **Tokugawa Ieyasu** moved his headquarters to Edo, he required a steady supply of high-quality fish for his castle. He turned to the skilled fishermen of Tsukuda-mura in Settsu Province (modern-day Osaka), who had previously aided him during his military campaigns. In 1590, thirty-three of these fishermen migrated to Edo. They were eventually granted a piece of land‚Äîa sandbar at the mouth of the Sumida River‚Äîwhich they reclaimed and named **Tsukudajima** after their hometown.

**The Divine Guardians: Sumiyoshi Sanjin**
In 1646, to protect their new home and ensure safe passage on the treacherous seas, the fishermen established this shrine as a branch of the grand **Sumiyoshi Taisha** in Osaka. They enshrined the **Sumiyoshi Sanjin** (the three deities of the sea and stars):
*   **Sokotsu-no-watatsumi-no-mikoto**
*   **Nakatsu-no-watatsumi-no-mikoto**
*   **Uwatsu-no-watatsumi-no-mikoto**

Alongside them, they enshrined **Emperor Ojin** and the spirit of the **Empress Jingu**. For nearly 400 years, the shrine has been worshipped as the ultimate protector of sailors, fishermen, and now, all those who live in the waterfront districts of Tokyo.

**The Birthplace of Tsukudani**
The shrine is also culturally significant as the birthplace of **Tsukudani** (small fish preserved in soy sauce and sugar). The fishermen created this durable food as a way to preserve their leftover catch for long voyages and as an offering to the shrine. Today, the surrounding streets are still home to shops that have sold Tsukudani since the Edo period.

### 2. ‚õ©Ô∏è A Stroll Through the Precincts: A Personal Photo Diary

Tsukuda is one of the few areas in central Tokyo that miraculously escaped both the Great Kanto Earthquake and the air raids of WWII. Walking toward the shrine via the iconic red **Tsukuda-kobashi Bridge**, you are transported back in time. The atmosphere is thick with nostalgia, with old wooden houses and narrow alleys that contrast sharply with the towering high-rises of the nearby "River City 21" complex.

**The Rare Ceramic 'Hengaku'**
A must-see feature of this shrine is the **Hengaku (calligraphy plaque)** hanging above the sanctuary. Unlike most shrine plaques which are made of wood, this one is made of **fired pottery (Oribe-yaki)**. It was donated in the Meiji era and is designated as a Tangible Cultural Property. Its intricate details and vibrant glaze are incredibly rare for a Shinto shrine.

### 3. üìú Goshuin and Shrine Items: Talismans of the Octagonal Mikoshi

**The Goshuin of Tsukuda**
The **Goshuin** (holy stamp) of Tsukuda Sumiyoshi Shrine is distinctive, often featuring a stamp that includes the shape of the shrine‚Äôs famous octagonal portable shrine (*Hakkaku Mikoshi*). During the triennial **Sumiyoshi Festival** (held once every three years in early August), limited-edition stamps are released that are highly coveted by collectors.

**Amulets for Safe Passage**
Because of its maritime heritage, the shrine offers unique **Kotsu Anzen** (Traffic Safety) and **Ryoko Anzen** (Travel Safety) amulets. These are particularly popular with those whose livelihoods depend on the sea or who are embarking on long overseas voyages. The "Hattorigata" amulets, which protect against general misfortune and "bad directions," are also a specialty of this shrine.
